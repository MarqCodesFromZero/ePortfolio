Software Engineering Project Management

This page brings together weekly artefacts, short reflections, and the three summative assessments for the Software Engineering Project Management module. The focus is practical: planning, estimating, risk, quality, and people. No fluff — just what it takes to deliver software on time, on budget, and up to scratch.

Main goals for the module

  1. Understand key principles of project management and how they relate to software engineering.
  2. Explore architecture, Behaviour-Driven Development (BDD), and SDLC models.
  3. Integrate approaches to manage conflicts and trade-offs across the triple constraint.
  4. Develop skills in analysis, design, software construction, and testing.
  5. Address psychological and sociological aspects of human–computer interaction.
  6. Reflect on personal development and anticipate future challenges in the field.

01 UNIT: Intro to SEPM

This unit introduced the Software Engineering Project Manager as a hybrid role, combining project management discipline with software engineering realities. I explored how planning, estimation, and resource coordination must adapt to the uncertainty and iterative nature of software development, rather than assuming stable scope and linear delivery.

Activity: Through a collaborative discussion on software project failure (Agrawal et al., 2024), I identified common failure patterns and linked them to weak early governance and stakeholder alignment.

Read more

The unit also emphasised that SEPM includes technical responsibilities such as requirements engineering, agile coordination, and collaborative design. A key takeaway was that project controls in software must be flexible enough to support change while still providing accountability and traceability for decisions.

At team level, we formalised a Group Contract defining roles, responsibilities, and communication rules, followed by an initial planning session to allocate tasks. These artefacts created a shared operating baseline and reduced coordination risk in a distributed team.

02 UNIT: Why Projects Fail

Focusing on the high rate of failure in software initiatives, I analysed case examples to identify root causes and mitigation strategies. A key outcome was understanding how clearer requirements engineering and stakeholder engagement reduce delivery risk.

Seminar: We practised eliciting requirements using Gherkin, turning vague user needs into structured, testable scenarios that support implementation and verification.

Read more

This unit reinforced that failure is often systemic: weak scoping, fragmented communication, and unmanaged risk tend to cascade into schedule slips, cost overruns, and quality degradation. Analysing peer examples helped me recognise how early warning signals are often visible if governance is in place.

Applying Gherkin strengthened my ability to reduce ambiguity by defining behaviours and acceptance criteria, improving traceability from needs to delivery. This also created a more realistic foundation for planning and estimation work in later units.

03 UNIT: Estimating & Planning

This unit examined tools used for effective decision-making in project management, focusing on estimation techniques and planning tools that fit different project contexts. The emphasis was on selecting approaches based on uncertainty, constraints, and delivery model.

Reflective Activity: I analysed an everyday online system (Wang et al., 2023) to understand how back-end data structures support front-end performance, linking technical design choices to planning assumptions.

Read more

The key learning was that estimation is not only a number; it is a justified argument based on scope clarity, known risks, dependencies, and assumptions. Planning tools become more valuable when they expose these assumptions rather than hiding uncertainty behind a single figure.

In our team meeting, we reviewed sprint progress and redistributed workload for the upcoming phase, ensuring that delivery goals remained aligned with the timeline and the evolving requirements baseline.

04 UNIT: Risk Assessment

Building on earlier concepts, I deepened my understanding of risk evaluation for large-scale software systems. I practised producing project estimates while accounting for contextual factors such as uncertainty, dependencies, and quality constraints.

Wiki Activity: I mapped risks identified in literature (Anton and Nucu, 2020) to SDLC stages and proposed practical mitigation strategies.

Read more

This unit strengthened my ability to categorise and prioritise risks, linking mitigation choices to lifecycle timing (prevention early, containment later). It also reinforced how risk exposure increases when estimation and planning ignore uncertainty or treat assumptions as facts.

Seminar preparation involved applying risk frameworks to a scenario and evaluating which controls were proportionate to likelihood and impact, ensuring that mitigation was realistic rather than generic.

05 UNIT: User Experience

This unit examined User Experience (UX) and the Project Manager’s role in enabling it throughout the lifecycle. I explored how UX can be planned, measured, and protected alongside technical requirements and delivery constraints.

Collaborative Discussion: We debated factors influencing UX and how stakeholder priorities and trade-offs shape the final product.

Read more

A key insight was that UX is not purely interface design; it is a delivery outcome shaped by requirements quality, performance, reliability, and the way change is managed. This connected naturally to risk and estimation, since rushed delivery often compromises user experience and adoption.

In our combined Week 4+5 meeting, we refined the backlog, clarified dependencies across time zones, and aligned sprint priorities with emerging constraints and technical challenges.

06 UNIT: TDD & UX

This unit connected technical quality practices with user-centred delivery. I explored Test-Driven Development (TDD) and considered how stakeholder emotions and expectations influence how “success” is judged in practice.

Formative Activity: I used Visual Studio Code to practise TDD with pytest, reinforcing how automated tests reduce defects and improve confidence in change.

Read more

A key takeaway was that testing is not just a developer concern: it is a project control that supports predictable delivery, protects UX, and reduces rework late in the lifecycle. This unit also highlighted that stakeholder reactions are shaped by perceived reliability, communication, and expectation management—not only by feature completion.

This week also marked submission of the Team Project Report, where I contributed to requirements analysis and cost estimation, ensuring alignment between scope, feasibility, and delivery constraints.

07 UNIT: SDLC

I compared Waterfall and Agile approaches to understand how the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) shapes project management work. Activities included structuring requirements using the Volere template and defining BDD scenarios.

Reflective Activity: I analysed peer contributions on SDLC practice and considered how architecture and lifecycle decisions interact with UX and project outcomes.

Read more

This unit clarified that lifecycle choice changes governance and change control: Waterfall relies on early stability, while Agile depends on disciplined iteration, prioritisation, and stakeholder engagement. The Volere template strengthened my ability to structure requirements in a way that remains useful even when scope evolves.

The BDD focus reinforced that “definition of done” should be testable and traceable, supporting both quality assurance and better expectation management.

08 UNIT: Data Structures

This unit focused on managing electronic data in connected systems. I selected and applied Python data structures (lists, dictionaries, sets) to support efficient retention, access, and transformation of data.

Seminar Prep: I justified structure choices against functional and non-functional requirements using academic sources (e.g., Abeykoon et al., 2020).

Read more

The main learning was that “correct” data structures depend on the access pattern and constraints: performance, maintainability, and correctness requirements often shape the best choice. This linked back to planning because architecture decisions affect scalability, effort, and risk.

The unit also reinforced communicating technical choices clearly, so stakeholders understand why specific implementations are needed to meet quality attributes such as latency and reliability.

09 UNIT: Quality Strategy

I explored the distinction between quality planning, control, and assurance, focusing on how non-functional requirements translate into measurable test criteria. I also examined technical debt as a predictable outcome of unmanaged quality trade-offs.

Activity: I applied code quality improvement strategies in Python, reinforcing how quality metrics and standards are implemented in practice.

Read more

This unit reinforced that quality needs governance: without shared standards and acceptance criteria, defects and rework become “normalised,” increasing cost and slowing delivery. Technical debt was particularly relevant as it links short-term delivery pressure to long-term maintenance burden.

Applying quality improvements in a real codebase demonstrated how planning quality upfront reduces failure risk later, supporting more predictable delivery and better user outcomes.

10 UNIT: Quality Monitoring

This unit focused on maintaining code standards through practical monitoring. I applied Python linters to automate quality checks and improve code hygiene, and examined how software quality concepts have evolved over time.

Formative Activity: I used tools such as Pylint, Pyflakes, and Pycodestyle to identify issues in a Python script, reflecting on how automated checks reduce project failure risk caused by poor quality.

Read more

The key learning was that automated quality gates reduce variation across teams and prevent quality drift during iteration. Tooling also supports maintainability by enforcing clearer structure, documentation, and consistent style.

The unit also explored recovery strategies when quality has already degraded, reinforcing that prevention is cheaper than remediation, but remediation is possible when approached systematically.

11 UNIT: Future Trends

I reviewed emerging trends in computer science and IT to consider their impact on the future of SEPM, focusing on how innovations may reshape planning, risk, governance, and stakeholder management.

Assessment 2: I prepared a recorded presentation arguing for one trend as a key driver of future SEPM practice, including risks, mitigations, and updated requirements.

Read more

This unit strengthened my ability to move from “trend awareness” to practical implications: how a trend changes team capability needs, delivery constraints, security and compliance risks, and how success should be measured.

The assessment required translating analysis into concrete project artefacts (requirements, costs, timeline, mitigation plan), reinforcing that future-focused SEPM must remain evidence-based and operational.

12 UNIT: The Future of SEPM

Synthesising research from Unit 11, I evaluated multiple trends and argued for the most influential driver shaping SEPM’s future direction, linking it to practical delivery outcomes.

Final Reflective Piece: Using the “What, So What, Now What” model, I identified key learning incidents from the module and defined how I will apply them in future projects.

Read more

The key learning was that future-facing SEPM requires balancing innovation with controls: governance, quality, and risk management remain essential even as tooling and delivery models change. Strong project management is still defined by clarity of requirements, disciplined execution, and stakeholder alignment.

The final reflection consolidated improvements in my practice: stronger requirements thinking, more realistic estimation, clearer risk framing, and a higher emphasis on measurable quality. These areas directly transfer into professional delivery contexts where deadlines, cost constraints, and stakeholder expectations must be managed in parallel.
