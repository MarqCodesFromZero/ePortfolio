Information Security & Risk Management

This module introduces core principles of information security and risk management. It explores frameworks for threat modelling, security standards, disaster recovery strategies, and emerging trends in the field. For each unit I have detailed my learning outcomes and artefacts.

The module is structured into six units:

  • Unit 1: Introduction to Security and Risk Management
  • Unit 2: Threat Modelling Exercises
  • Unit 3: Introduction to Threat Modelling and Management
  • Unit 4: Security Standards, Frameworks and Disaster Recovery
  • Unit 5: Future Trends in Security and Risk Management
  • Unit 6: The Great Debate – Most Influential Trend in ISM (Next 5 Years)

Unit 3: Threat Modelling Techniques and Framework Selection

This week focused on exploring different approaches to threat modelling and the decision-making process behind selecting a suitable framework. We discussed distinctions between threats and vulnerabilities and reviewed techniques such as STRIDE, DREAD, Attack Trees, and hybrid methods like PASTA. Resources such as OWASP’s Threat Modelling Cookbook were also introduced to support structured modelling decisions.

By the end of the unit, I was able to describe and compare multiple threat modelling techniques, determine their best-use scenarios, and understand how to combine them when appropriate to better support real-world system design and evaluation.

Activity: Standards Evaluation for Pampered Pets

For this exercise, I analysed the relevance of security and privacy standards—GDPR (ICO, 2020), PCI-DSS (PCI SSC, 2020), and HIPAA (HIPAA, 2020)—to a fictional pet-care business. I evaluated the organisation’s processes and determined which regulations applied, how compliance could be verified, and what measures should be implemented.

I recommended GDPR compliance for handling customer and pet data, and PCI-DSS for secure payment processing. I assumed the business does not handle protected health information, so HIPAA was not deemed applicable. I also proposed a periodic compliance audit and staff training as part of ongoing controls.

Unit 4: Security Standards, Frameworks and Disaster Recovery

This week focused on the strategic role of security standards in risk assessments and the importance of disaster recovery (DR) in ensuring business continuity. Topics included selecting appropriate frameworks for various industries, as well as DR planning concepts like Recovery Time Objective (RTO), Recovery Point Objective (RPO), and deployment types such as hot, warm, and cold standby.

By the end of the unit, I was confident in identifying applicable industry standards for specific organisations and outlining DR strategies based on system needs and operational constraints.

Wiki Activity: Security Frameworks

Based on Kirvan (2021), I assessed twelve common security frameworks and matched them to sector-specific needs:

  • International Bank: ISO/IEC 27001 and NIST CSF – for risk-based ISMS and adaptive incident response.
  • Large Hospital: HITRUST CSF and ISO/IEC 27001 – ensuring HIPAA compliance and full governance beyond healthcare-specific systems.
  • Large Food Manufacturer: NIST CSF and ISO/IEC 27001 – for IT/OT integration, SCADA system protection, and enterprise-wide security controls.

For each case, I recommended key tests such as maturity assessments, gap analyses, and network segmentation reviews. Implementation priorities were drawn around encryption, access controls, compliance audits, and security awareness programmes.

Seminar Exercise: Disaster Recovery Solutions

Through three exercises, I applied DR concepts to technical and strategic design:

  • Activity 1: Compared hot, warm, and cold standby in terms of RTO/RPO using Abualkishik et al. (2020). Highlighted how virtualisation and clustering improve recovery options.
  • Activity 2: Used Morrow et al. (2021) to explore vendor lock-in and security risks in cloud DR. Recommended mitigation via cloud-agnostic tools, encryption, and role-based access.
  • Activity 3: Created three DR diagrams for different RTO/RPO scenarios, using clustered and standby systems based on availability needs.

Unit 5: Future Trends in Security and Risk Management

This unit reviewed current and emerging trends in security and risk management, exploring topics such as AI, automation, economic factors, and their implications on future security strategies. The seminar and readings prompted critical evaluation of these trends in relation to future directions in the field.

By the end of the unit, I was able to assess several emerging technologies—like Automated Cyber Defence (ACD)—and their impact on core risk management principles such as explainability, transparency, and adaptability. I explored how evolving technologies both support and complicate foundational frameworks in risk representation.

Wiki Entry: The Evolving Role of Automated Cyber Defence

My contribution to the collaborative wiki focused on Terje Aven’s (2016) call for transparency in risk assessment. I examined this concept through the lens of Automated Cyber Defence (ACD), highlighting challenges such as explainability, the "reality gap," and evolving AI-based threats. Drawing on Vyas et al. (2023), I proposed strategies including Explainable AI, assumption management and continuous monitoring to support clearer and more trustworthy risk representations in the future.

Unit 6: Integrative Practice & Future Trends Debate

The final unit consolidated everything learned throughout the module and challenged us to apply Security & Risk Management (SRM) techniques in two ways: a Python-based Attack-Tree App for the Pampered Pets case study, and a group debate on the trend most likely to shape SRM over the next five years, where our team argued for Content-Centric / Named-Data Networking (CCN/NDN). The unit closed with a reflective essay on my overall learning journey.

Assignment 2: Pampered Pets Attack-Tree App

Debate Presentation: Why CCN/NDN Will Redefine SRM (2025-2030)

In the live seminar our team demonstrated how data-centric naming, intrinsic signatures and in-network caching can shift SRM from perimeter-centric to content-centric security. We compared CCN/NDN to P2P overlays and MobilityFirst, concluding that CCN/NDN offers the broadest, most transparent impact on data integrity, provenance and DDoS resilience.
